What to Say When Someone Interrupts You in a Meeting

Don't compete on volume. Five steps for handling interruptions in real time without escalating the moment or surrendering the floor.

Updated April 25, 2026 · By the DeftBrain team

You started talking. You got two sentences in. Someone cut you off — finished your thought for you, redirected the topic, or just talked over you until you stopped. The room moved on. Now you're sitting there with the rest of what you were going to say, deciding whether to push back in real time, raise it later, or pretend it didn't happen.

Interruptions in meetings are a specific challenge because the response window is narrow — a few seconds — and competing for the floor on volume usually loses you the room even when you win the moment. The techniques below are designed for that specific window: short phrases that hold the floor without escalating, calibrated to the kind of interruption you're dealing with. There's also the harder question, which is when an individual interruption is part of a pattern that needs addressing outside the meeting itself.

How to do it
1

Don't compete on volume — name what's happening

The reflexive response to being interrupted is to talk louder, faster, or more aggressively to claim the floor back. This usually loses. The room reads escalation as the response of someone who couldn't hold the floor with their content alone, and the interrupter often gets credit for being the more confident speaker even when they were the one who broke the conversation. The technique that works better is short, calm naming. 'Let me finish that thought.' 'Hold on, I want to land that point.' 'I wasn't done — give me thirty seconds.' Each of these is a sentence, not a confrontation. The room reads it as composure. The interrupter has to either yield the floor (which restores the order) or interrupt the calm naming (which makes the pattern visible to everyone in the room and costs them credibility, not you). Naming is more effective than competing because it relies on the implicit social agreement that one person speaks at a time, which everyone in the room is already party to.

What to say

"Let me land this point — I think the next part actually answers what you just asked."

This is one of the highest-success-rate phrases for reclaiming the floor in real time. Three things make it work. It's calm and short (no volume escalation). It uses position language ('let me land this point' is about the conversational state, not the interrupter's behavior). And it offers the interrupter a face-saving frame ('the next part answers what you asked'), which lets them yield without feeling like they were corrected. Most interrupters yield to this within a sentence. The phrasing internalizes well as a default, which is what you want — the response window is too narrow to compose something fresh.

2

Use position language, not personality language

There's a difference between 'you're being rude' (personality) and 'I wasn't finished' (position). Personality language invites argument about whether you're correct in your characterization, which is the kind of meta-conversation that derails the meeting and rarely lands in your favor. Position language is harder to argue with because it's a statement of fact: you were speaking, you weren't finished. The interrupter can't dispute that without explicitly continuing to interrupt, which makes the dynamic more obvious to the room. Position language also doesn't escalate — it doesn't accuse, doesn't characterize, doesn't make a claim about the interrupter's motives. It just describes what was happening. The discipline is to keep the language at the level of what was happening in the conversation, not at the level of why the interrupter did it. The 'why' is often unknowable; the 'what' is observable, and observable is what you can speak to without picking a fight.

3

Hold the floor with one specific phrase

The phrases that work to reclaim the floor are short, clean, and don't sound rehearsed. Three reliable options. 'Let me land this point' — works in most contexts, signals the speaker has somewhere they're going. 'I want to finish — I think the next part is the answer to your question' — works when the interruption was a tangent that may have been preempting your conclusion. 'Hold on, give me thirty seconds' — works when you need to ask for explicit airtime, useful in faster-paced meetings. Each is one sentence. Each sounds natural in conversation rather than performative. Pick one, internalize it as your default, and use it. Having a default phrase ready matters because the response window for handling interruptions is too narrow to compose a phrase from scratch — you need something that comes out automatically when you need it, the way 'no, thank you' comes out without thought when someone offers you something you don't want.

4

Decide whether the interruption is a pattern that needs addressing offline

Single interruptions from most people are usually one-off — a moment of poor self-monitoring, an excited colleague, a faster-paced meeting culture than you're used to. These don't need addressing beyond the in-the-moment handling. Some interruptions are part of a pattern — the same person interrupting you specifically, in ways that don't happen to others in the room or that don't happen to other men or other women in the room. The third or fourth instance is no longer a one-off; it's signal. The right response to a pattern isn't more in-the-moment naming, which doesn't change the underlying dynamic. It's a private conversation outside the meeting: 'I've noticed I get interrupted by you more than other people in our standups. I want to talk about it.' That conversation often goes better than people fear, because most pattern-level interrupters aren't aware of the pattern, and being given the chance to course-correct privately is usually received well. The signal you've reached pattern territory: are you adjusting your speaking behavior around this person specifically? If yes, it's a pattern.

5

Know when the interruption isn't actually the problem

There's a category of meeting where the interruptions you're noticing aren't the underlying issue — they're a symptom of something larger. The pattern: the meeting is structured in a way that incentivizes interruption (no clear facilitation, time pressure, multiple speakers competing for limited airtime), or the room dynamics reflect a hierarchy that's being expressed through who interrupts whom, or your role in the meeting is being defined as a participant whose input is optional rather than required. In these cases, in-the-moment interruption-handling treats the symptom and leaves the cause in place. The remedy is structural rather than tactical. That might mean talking to the meeting facilitator about adding turn-taking norms, talking to your manager about your role and standing in these meetings, or stepping back to ask whether this meeting is actually serving you given how it's run. The signal: handling interruptions feels like the same fight every week with the same outcome. If yes, the fight isn't the right battle — the meeting structure is. Sometimes the most useful response to chronic interruptions is to change the conversation about the meeting itself, not the conversation in it.
