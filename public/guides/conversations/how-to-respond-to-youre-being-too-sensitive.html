How to Respond to "You're Being Too Sensitive" Without Proving Them Right

It's the move that shifts the topic from their behavior to your reaction. Five steps for not falling into the trap, even when you're already in the middle of it.

Updated April 25, 2026 · By the DeftBrain team

You said something. They said something back. The thing they said back is some version of: 'you're being too sensitive,' 'why are you making such a big deal of this,' 'I was just joking, calm down,' 'you can't take a joke.' The conversation has now shifted away from whatever was originally said and toward whether you're overreacting. You can feel the trap close, and you can also feel that fighting your way out of it is likely to make it worse — because the harder you push back, the more your reaction becomes the topic.

'You're being too sensitive' is a specific conversational move with a specific function. It changes the subject of the conversation from the speaker's behavior to the listener's reaction. The original comment, whatever it was, becomes the unaddressed item in the room while everyone now considers whether you're handling it correctly. The move works best on people who try to defend themselves — because every defense reinforces the new framing. The five steps below are about not taking the bait, while still addressing what was originally said.

How to do it
1

Recognize the move — it shifts the topic from their behavior to your reaction

The first move is the cognitive one: name what just happened. The other person did something — said a comment, made a joke, took a shot — and when you registered it, they shifted the topic to whether your registration was warranted. The original event has now disappeared from the conversation. This is a specific move, not a random response. It's worth recognizing because the next steps depend on it. If you fight the new framing directly ('I'm not being too sensitive'), you're now arguing about your reaction, which is exactly where the move wanted you. If you accept the new framing ('maybe I am'), you've conceded the original point. The third option — neither fighting nor accepting — requires recognizing that the framing itself is the problem. Once you see the move clearly, the rest of the response shapes around getting back to the original topic instead of arguing about the new one.

2

Reframe back to the behavior, not the reaction

The technique that breaks the move is to redirect the conversation from your reaction back to the behavior that produced it — calmly, without escalating. 'I'm not asking whether I'm sensitive. I'm telling you what you said wasn't okay.' Or: 'Whether I'm being sensitive isn't the question — the question is what you said.' Each is a one-line move that refuses the new framing without arguing within it. The discipline is keeping the language at the level of behavior. 'What you said wasn't okay' is about the behavior. 'You're being a jerk' is about the person and invites a fresh argument about whether you're characterizing them correctly. Behavior-level language is much harder to deflect from. The speaker can't argue you didn't hear what you heard; they can only argue about whether what they said was wrong, which is the conversation that needed to happen in the first place.

What to say

"I'm not asking whether I'm sensitive — I'm telling you what you said wasn't okay. Can we talk about that?"

Two sentences that do the structural work of breaking the deflection. The first refuses the new framing without arguing within it; the second pivots back to the original behavior with an explicit invitation to engage with it. Calm, short, hard to argue with. Most deflectors take the off-ramp this offers because the alternative — continuing to argue you're too sensitive after you've explicitly named the move — makes the strategy visible to anyone watching. The phrasing internalizes well as a default response, which matters because the response window when this move happens is short.

3

Don't defend your reaction

The strongest pull during this exchange is to explain why you reacted the way you did. 'Well, I reacted because I'm tired and that comment hit a sore spot' or 'I'm not usually like this, but the way you said it…' Each of these defenses is honest, and each of them keeps the conversation on your reaction, which is the move's victory condition. The discipline is to refuse to defend. Your reaction doesn't need to be justified; the original behavior needs to be addressed. Even if you were genuinely overreacting, the conversation about that should come after the conversation about the underlying behavior, not instead of it. The phrase that helps: 'My reaction isn't the topic.' Said calmly, it's hard to argue with. Said heatedly, it sounds like denial. The temperature matters more than the content here, because the move feeds on heat — the calmer your refusal to defend, the harder the move is to sustain.

4

Don't escalate; reset

Once you've recognized the move and refused the framing, the most effective continuation is often a reset rather than an escalation. The reset is short and pragmatic. 'Let's just go back to what you said and address it. Was it intentional, or did you mean it differently?' This does two things at once: it puts the original topic back in the room as the agenda item, and it offers the speaker a face-saving frame for either acknowledging the comment was off ('didn't mean it that way') or owning it ('yeah, I meant it'). Either response is workable; both are improvements over continuing to argue about your sensitivity. The reset works because it treats the deflection as if it were a one-time misstep rather than a deliberate strategy, which gives the speaker the option to drop the strategy without losing face. Most do. The ones who don't reveal that the deflection wasn't accidental, which is information you needed to have anyway.

5

Know when this pattern is signaling something deeper about the relationship

There's a category of 'you're being too sensitive' that's a one-time deflection — a moment of bad behavior in an otherwise functional relationship — and a category that's a sustained pattern. The first is recoverable through the techniques above. The second isn't. The signal you've reached the second category: the same person uses this move on you regularly, you've come to expect it, and you've started either avoiding subjects that might trigger it or pre-rehearsing how you'll handle the next instance. At that point, the issue isn't the individual deflections; it's that the relationship has settled into a dynamic where your concerns don't get addressed because they get re-routed every time. This dynamic doesn't fix itself through better in-the-moment technique. It requires either a structural conversation outside the moments themselves ('I've noticed we keep ending up here, and I want to talk about it') or a decision about whether the relationship can hold a more honest version of itself. Sometimes the answer is yes, with work; sometimes the answer is no, and the recognition that 'no' is an option is the most useful information the pattern was offering. Better technique handles individual moments; relationship-level patterns need relationship-level conversations, not better lines.
