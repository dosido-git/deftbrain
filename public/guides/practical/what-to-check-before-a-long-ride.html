What to Check Before a Long Ride (The 10-Minute Inspection That Prevents Roadside Misery)

Most ride-ending mechanical problems were detectable in the garage that morning. A 10-minute pre-ride check catches the issues that would otherwise leave you stranded — and the items to check are surprisingly few.

Updated April 27, 2026 · By the DeftBrain team

You're planning a longer ride than usual — a century, a multi-hour mountain bike loop, a tour through unfamiliar terrain, a destination ride somewhere remote. The bike has been working fine on shorter rides, but you have a vague awareness that 'fine for an hour' isn't the same as 'fine for six hours.' Small issues that don't matter on a quick ride can become ride-enders thirty miles from home. You don't want to over-prepare. You also don't want to be stranded.

The pre-ride inspection for longer rides is short and specific — about ten minutes covering the handful of items that, when neglected, are most likely to cause trouble far from help. The same checks aren't necessary before every casual ride, but they're worth doing before any ride where mechanical failure would mean hours of walking or an expensive ride home. The list is shorter than most cycling content suggests, and the goal isn't paranoia; it's catching the obvious problems that are usually visible if you specifically look.

How to do it
1

Tire pressure, tire condition, and the spare tube

Check pressure on both tires using a real gauge — not just feel — and inflate to your target pressure for the conditions and surface. Look at the tire surface for any embedded debris, cuts, or worn spots, especially on the rear tire which wears faster. Check the spare tube in your kit isn't dry-rotted or damaged (squeeze it to feel for stiffness or cracks) and confirm your CO2 cartridges or pump are present and functional. This entire check takes two minutes and prevents the most common ride-ending problem. A flat thirty miles into a ride is meaningfully different from a flat three miles in — having the kit functional matters disproportionately.

2

Quick-release skewers, thru-axles, and bolts that hold the bike together

Verify both quick-release levers (or thru-axles) are fully closed and properly tensioned — they should require firm pressure to close, with the lever ending in a position where it can't be casually bumped open. Check the stem and handlebar bolts for visible looseness; you can usually tell by trying to twist the handlebars relative to the front wheel. Check the seatpost clamp by trying to twist the saddle. Check pedal tightness if you've recently swapped pedals. The 30-second sanity check on the major bolts catches the rare but serious problems where something works loose and creates an actual safety issue. This is the kind of check whose value is in the once-in-a-thousand-rides finding, not the routine confirmation.

3

Shifting and braking through every gear and brake

Lift the rear wheel briefly and shift through every rear gear while pedaling slowly with your hand. The chain should move quickly and quietly to each cog. Do the same with the front shifter. Squeeze each brake lever — both should reach a firm stopping point with significant lever travel remaining. If shifting is hesitant or braking feels weak, address it before the ride, not during. These problems rarely fix themselves, and a slow-shifting bike or weak brakes that are tolerable on a short ride become exhausting and potentially dangerous over many hours.

4

The kit you're carrying — confirm everything is present

Open your saddle bag or hip pack and physically verify the contents. Spare tube. Tire levers. CO2 or pump. Multi-tool. Patch kit. Chain quick-link if you carry one. Plus the ride-specific items: enough water and food for the planned distance, lights if there's any chance you'll be out near dusk, an ID and emergency contact info, your phone with adequate battery. The verification takes a minute. The frequency of stranded riders who realized mid-ride that they'd left a critical item at home — or that an item had migrated out of their bag without them noticing — is high enough that the check is worth doing every time.

5

When the pre-ride check finds something — the right calibration on whether to ride anyway

Sometimes the inspection finds a small issue. The decision then is whether to fix it before riding, fix it after, or just live with it. A guideline that holds across most cases: any issue that affects safety (loose bolts, weak brakes, frayed cables) gets fixed before the ride, even if it means cutting the ride short or skipping it. Issues that affect performance but not safety (slightly noisy chain, mildly imprecise shifting) can usually wait until later. Issues you've been ignoring for weeks that you suddenly notice in the pre-ride check are worth addressing now, partly because the upcoming ride is exactly the situation where they're most likely to fail. The pre-ride check works best when you treat it as a real input to whether and how to ride, not as a formality you do before riding regardless. The rider who finds a problem and addresses it is the rider who has fewer mid-ride problems. The rider who finds a problem and rides anyway — counting on luck — is the rider you sometimes see walking back to their car. Be the first one.
