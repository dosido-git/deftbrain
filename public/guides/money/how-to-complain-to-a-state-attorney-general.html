How to Complain to a State Attorney General (When the Company Has Crossed a Legal Line)

Your state attorney general's office has real enforcement authority — and a complaint there triggers a different response than a complaint to the company itself. Knowing when it's the right move and how to file is a meaningful piece of consumer leverage.

Updated April 27, 2026 · By the DeftBrain team

You've gone through three rounds of customer service. You've sent the formal complaint letter. You've considered a chargeback. The company has dug in. The dispute is, by your read, no longer about a misunderstanding — it's about a pattern of behavior that probably isn't legal, and the company knows it isn't legal but has decided you don't have the leverage to do anything about it.

Your state attorney general's consumer protection division exists for exactly this case. Filing a complaint there isn't theatrical and it isn't a threat — it's a real procedural move that triggers a real response. AGs investigate consumer protection violations, force companies to respond in writing, and have the authority to levy fines, file lawsuits, and shut down operations. Most AG complaints don't lead to enforcement action against a single company, but they do produce written responses from companies that had been ignoring you, and they're aggregated to identify patterns that lead to bigger investigations. The complaint takes 20 minutes to file. The leverage it generates is disproportionate to the effort.

How to do it
1

Know what kinds of complaints AGs actually take

State AGs handle consumer protection violations: deceptive advertising, fraud, contract violations, predatory lending, debt collection abuse, illegal billing practices, and discrimination. They generally do not handle disputes that are simply 'I don't think the company treated me fairly' if no law was actually broken. The threshold is whether the company's behavior crosses into deception, illegality, or pattern abuse — not whether it merely disappointed you. If your complaint involves a specific law you believe was violated, or a clearly deceptive practice, AG is the right venue. If it's about service quality or one-off bad behavior, regulators are usually the better path.

2

File in your state, not the company's state

Counterintuitive but important: you file with your own state's AG, not the AG of the state where the company is headquartered. Your state has jurisdiction because the company did business in your state by selling to you. This matters because state AGs have widely varying activity levels — some have aggressive consumer protection divisions (California, New York, Massachusetts, Washington), some have minimal staff and slow processes, but all have at least the basic procedural authority to forward your complaint and require a response. File where you live, where the harm occurred, regardless of where the company is incorporated.

3

Use the online complaint form, even if it feels small

Most state AG offices have moved to online complaint forms — usually findable by searching '[state] attorney general consumer complaint.' The form is short, takes 15-20 minutes, and asks for a chronology of events, supporting documentation, and the resolution you're seeking. Don't underestimate the form because it looks simple. Behind the simple interface is a process that catalogs your complaint, often forwards it to the company within a few weeks, and aggregates it with other complaints to identify patterns. The form being short doesn't mean the response is.

4

Frame the complaint around what was illegal, not what was annoying

AG offices read complaints with one main question: was a law or regulation likely violated? Frame your complaint to answer that question. 'The company billed me $200 after I canceled, in violation of [state] consumer protection statute requiring honoring of cancellations.' 'The company advertised a 30-day money-back guarantee but refused to honor it after 14 days, which appears to be deceptive advertising.' 'The debt collector continued contact after I sent a written cease-and-desist, in violation of the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act.' Specific violations get specific attention; general grievances get filed.

5

When the AG complaint is the move that ends the dispute

Here's what often happens after you file. Within 2-6 weeks, the AG's office sends a copy of your complaint to the company and asks for a written response. Companies have to take this seriously — non-responses get noted in the AG's records and can become evidence in larger investigations. The company assigns the case to its legal or executive team, not regular customer service. They write a response, usually offering a resolution they had previously refused. The AG forwards the response to you. You decide whether to accept. About 60-70% of consumer AG complaints resolve at this stage without any further enforcement action — not because the AG forced anything, but because the company decided that fighting an AG-flagged complaint was more expensive than just resolving it. Which is the leverage you came in for.
